Stats 115 Final Presentation: Drug Use in Young Adults

Group 14: Melody Fang, Andrew Liu, Hasnain Sizar, Tyler Tran

Motivation: Why Does This Matter?

  • Alcohol Use Disorder affects ~29 million Americans (SAMHSA, 2024)
  • Economic cost of excessive alcohol use exceeds $249 billion annually
  • State-level variation means policy solutions are not one-size-fits-all

Why Bayesian?

  • Prior research gives us genuine information about expected rates by age — wasteful to ignore
  • We get full posterior uncertainty, not just point estimates

Data Description

  • Source: NSDUH (National Survey on Drug Use and Health) conducted by SAMHSA
  • Time Range: 2002-2018 | Scope: All 50 US States
  • Survey Population: Aged 12+ non institutionalized US civilian residents
    • Excludes active-duty military, institutionalized residents, those without addresses
  • Data Collection: Confidential, in-person computer-assisted interviews, no names collected, to encourage honest reporting
  • Units: Totals in thousands of people; rates are per 1000 people

Problem/Question Description

  • Across different age groups, which state (out of the top 10 with the highest populations in the United States) has the highest rate of alcohol usage disorder?
  • Which age group has the overall highest rate?

Prior Description

  • Weakly informative priors
  • Rates are measured per 1,000 people
  • Expected age pattern: 12–17 < 26+ < 18–25
  • 10 states as groups → hierarchical model with partial pooling across states

Intercept priors (means from 2023–2024 SAMHSA averages):

Age Group Prior Reasoning
12–17 normal(30, 15) Wide SD reflects highly variable adolescent environments
18–25 normal(140, 30) Widest SD reflects high cultural variability in young adults
26+ normal(60, 20) Moderate SD; more stabilized adult drinking behavior
  • Residual SD: prior_aux = exponential(1, autoscale = TRUE)
  • Between-state variability: prior_covariance = decov(1, 1, 1, 1)

Initial Plotting

  • The red line marks the overall mean across all 5 states for each age group
  • Rates are the lowest across all age groups for 12-17 and highest for 18-25, which is consistent with national literature on adolescent alcohol use disorder
  • States cluster near the global mean → motivates partial pooling via hierarchical model

Model Diagnostics

  • PP check confirms the model captures center and spread of observed rates well for all 3 models
  • MCMC trace plots and ACF confirmed good chain mixing and convergence for all models

Age Group 1 Credible & Predictive Intervals

  • Credible intervals are narrow and overlapping — states are similar at this age
  • All observed means fall within their predictive intervals — good model calibration
  • Wide intervals reflect genuine year-to-year unpredictability at this age group

Age Group 2 Credible & Predictive Intervals

  • Credible intervals show more separation between states than in the 12–17 group — real state-level differences are detectable here
  • Observed means fall within predictive intervals — model is well-calibrated
  • Wider intervals than 12–17, reflecting greater absolute variability at higher rates

Age Group 3 Credible & Predictive Intervals

  • State-level differences remain visible but intervals are tighter than 18–25, reflecting more stable adult behavior
  • Narrowest predictive intervals of all three groups — most consistent year-to-year rates
  • All observed means fall within their predictive intervals

  • 18–25 dominates every state — credible intervals never overlap with other groups
  • Table shows posterior probability each state is the highest — a direct Bayesian answer, no p-values needed

Shrinkage, Conclusion & Future Directions

Shrinkage: arrows show raw state means pulled toward the global mean via partial pooling — extreme values are moderated, especially for noisier states

Conclusions:

  • 18–25 has the highest AUD rates universally
  • Top states vary by age group but PA and IL appear near the top consistently
  • Hierarchical model with partial pooling was the right choice — no state is a dramatic outlier once pooling is applied

Limitations & Future Work:

  • Data ends 2018 — post-pandemic trends are unaccounted for
  • State aggregation hides demographic variation (income, race, urbanicity)
  • Future: add time trend, demographic covariates, expand to all substances
